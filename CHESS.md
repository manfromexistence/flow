

# 20 Advanced Chess Rules to Reach GM Level

---

## Rule #1: The Principle of Two Weaknesses

One weakness is defendable. Create a **second weakness** on the opposite side of the board, then alternate pressure between both. Your opponent's pieces can't stretch across the whole board forever. Something will collapse.

**Study:** Karpov's games — he was the absolute king of this.

---

## Rule #2: Prophylaxis (Stop Their Idea FIRST)

Before making your move, ask **"What does my opponent WANT to do next?"** If their idea is dangerous, **prevent it first**, then continue your plan. Most players below 2200 only think about their own plans. GMs think about BOTH sides every single move.

**Study:** Petrosian's games — the greatest prophylactic player ever.

---

## Rule #3: Improve Your Worst Piece

Every position has one piece that is doing the least work. Find it. **Reroute it to a better square.** When all your pieces are on their best possible squares, the position often wins itself. Don't start an attack with a passive piece sitting in the corner doing nothing.

**Test yourself:** Every move, scan all your pieces and ask *"Which one is my worst piece right now?"*

---

## Rule #4: Never Rush a Won Position

When you have a clear advantage, **slow down.** Don't look for a quick kill. Instead, improve your position piece by piece, take away all counterplay, THEN deliver the final blow. More games are thrown away by rushing a winning position than by being outplayed from an equal one.

**Mantra:** *"Make it harder for my opponent to do ANYTHING before I try to finish the game."*

---

## Rule #5: Pawn Structure Dictates Your Plan

Don't search for random plans. **Look at the pawn structure and it will TELL you the plan.** Every major pawn structure has known plans that have been tested for over a century.

- IQP → attack with pieces, use the outpost in front of the isolated pawn
- Carlsbad → minority attack on the queenside OR kingside attack depending on your side
- French structure → White attacks kingside, Black attacks queenside
- Maroczy Bind → squeeze, prevent d5 break

**Learn 10-12 major pawn structures and their plans.** This alone will jump you 200 rating points.

---

## Rule #6: Zwischenzug (The In-Between Move)

In any tactical sequence, before making the "obvious" recapture or expected move, ask **"Is there a stronger move I can insert FIRST?"** A check, a threat, an attack on a hanging piece — slipped in BETWEEN the expected moves. This is how masters win pieces and games that look equal.

**Training:** When solving tactics, always ask *"What if I DON'T recapture immediately?"*

---

## Rule #7: Trade Pieces When You Have a Static Advantage

If you have a better pawn structure, a passed pawn, or a better bishop — **trade pieces.** Fewer pieces on the board magnify static advantages. The endgame will be winning almost by itself.

**Opposite rule:** If your advantage is DYNAMIC (initiative, development lead, attack) — **avoid trades** because your advantage disappears with fewer pieces.

---

## Rule #8: The Principle of the Worst Square for the Opponent's Piece

When you have a choice of where to push your opponent's piece (through threats or attacks), always push it to **its worst possible square**, not just any square. Don't just chase a knight away — chase it to a square where it's completely useless.

---

## Rule #9: Create Passed Pawns on the OPPOSITE Side of the King

A passed pawn on the same side as the kings is often blockable. A passed pawn on the **opposite side** forces the opponent's king to choose — stop the pawn or stay near the action. This dilemma is often decisive in endgames.

---

## Rule #10: Rook Activity Over Material

In rook endgames especially, **an active rook is worth more than a pawn.** Never make your rook passive just to hold one pawn. A passive rook is a losing rook. Get your rook behind passed pawns (yours OR your opponent's), on open files, on the 7th rank — activity is everything.

**Study:** Rubinstein's rook endgames.

---

## Rule #11: The Principle of Maximum Difficulty

When defending a worse position, **don't just try to hold.** Make every move as difficult as possible for your opponent. Create complications, pose problems, force them to find precise moves. Even GMs crack under pressure when they have to find 10 accurate moves in a row.

**Mindset:** You're not trying to be "okay." You're trying to make your opponent EARN the win move by move.

---

## Rule #12: Control Key Squares, Not Just Pieces

Squares matter more than pieces in many positions. **Controlling one key outpost** (like e5, d5, c4) with a knight or rook can completely paralyze your opponent's entire position. Think about SQUARES first, then figure out which piece belongs there.

---

## Rule #13: Opposite-Colored Bishops Favor the Attacker in the Middlegame

Most players think opposite-colored bishops = draw. That is ONLY true in endgames. In the **middlegame**, opposite-colored bishops massively favor the attacker because the defender's bishop **cannot contest your bishop's diagonals.** It's almost like playing with an extra piece on the attack.

---

## Rule #14: Don't Move Pawns in Front of Your King Without a Reason

Every pawn move in front of your king **creates permanent weaknesses.** h3, g3, f3 — each one creates holes that can never be repaired. Only push these pawns if you have a concrete reason (preventing a pin, fianchetto, creating luft). Random pawn moves in front of your king are invitations for your opponent's pieces.

---

## Rule #15: The Minority Attack

When you have fewer pawns on one side (say 2 vs 3 on the queenside), **advance your minority to create a weakness in your opponent's majority.** Push b4-b5 to force a trade or create an isolated or backward pawn in their camp. This is a standard winning strategy in many QGD and Carlsbad structures.

---

## Rule #16: Restrict Before You Attack

Before launching an attack, **take away your opponent's counterplay and defensive resources first.** Close down their active possibilities, restrict their pieces, control open files. THEN attack. An attack against an opponent who has no counterplay is 10x more effective than one where they can hit back.

**Think of it as:** Tie their hands, THEN punch.

---

## Rule #17: The Bishop Pair is a LONG-TERM Advantage

Two bishops vs bishop and knight or two knights is not immediately decisive. But as the position opens up (pawns get traded, files open), the bishop pair becomes **increasingly powerful.** If you have the bishop pair, **open the position.** If your opponent has it, **keep it closed.**

---

## Rule #18: Centralize Everything Before Endgames

Before the position transitions into an endgame, **centralize your king and your pieces.** A centralized king in the endgame is worth almost a full piece of advantage. Many players leave their king on g1 while transitioning to an endgame — that is a huge mistake. Start marching it to the center early.

---

## Rule #19: Every Move Must Either Improve Your Position or Worsen Your Opponent's

If your move does **neither** of these things, it is a wasted move. Every single tempo matters. Ask yourself before every move: *"Is this making my position better? Is this making their position worse?"* If the answer is no to both, find a different move. No neutral moves.

---

## Rule #20: Study Your LOSSES More Than Your Wins

Your wins teach you almost nothing. Your **losses contain every weakness you have.** After every loss, analyze without an engine first — write down where you think you went wrong, what your thought process was, where you felt lost. THEN check with an engine. Pattern match your recurring mistakes. Fix one weakness at a time.

**The uncomfortable truth:** Your rating is determined by the frequency of your worst moves, not your best ones.
